Game theory · Fairness · Behavioral economics

Ultimatum Game

A game about why people do not always maximize money alone.

Scenario

Two players must divide $100.

The first player (the proposer) chooses how to split the money between themselves and the second player (the responder).

The responder then chooses whether to accept or reject the offer.

  • If the responder accepts, both players receive the proposed amounts.
  • If the responder rejects, both players receive nothing.
What offer should the proposer make?
Suppose You Are the Responder

The proposer offers you $1 and keeps $99.

If you reject the offer, both of you receive nothing.

Would you accept or reject the offer?
Reveal

From a purely financial perspective, accepting is the rational choice. Receiving $1 is better than receiving nothing.

Complete Explanation

If the responder is interested only in maximizing the amount of money they receive, they should accept any positive offer.

Knowing this, classical game theory predicts that the proposer should offer the smallest possible amount while keeping nearly everything.

Real experiments consistently produce a different result.

Responders frequently reject offers they consider unfair, even though doing so leaves them with nothing. Anticipating this, proposers often offer much larger amounts—commonly around 40–50% of the total.

The Ultimatum Game demonstrates that human decision-making is influenced by much more than financial payoff. People often value fairness, reciprocity, and the willingness to punish unfair behavior, even at a personal cost.

Rather than asking what is mathematically optimal, the game reveals how social values shape economic decisions.
