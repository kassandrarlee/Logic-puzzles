100 Prisoners and the Lightbulb

A puzzle about coordinating reliable information with only a single shared signal.

Scenario

One hundred prisoners are held in separate cells with no means of communication.

Each day, one prisoner is selected uniformly at random and taken to a room containing a single lightbulb. Afterward, they are returned to their cell.

Each prisoner will be selected many times over the course of the experiment, but the order is completely random and unpredictable.

The lightbulb can be either on or off. Whenever a prisoner enters the room, they may observe the current state of the lightbulb and choose whether to change it before leaving.

At any time, any prisoner may announce that every prisoner has visited the room at least once.

  • If the announcement is correct, all prisoners are set free.
  • If the announcement is incorrect, all prisoners are executed.

Before the process begins, the prisoners are allowed to meet once and agree on a strategy. After that, they may never communicate again.

Can the prisoners devise a strategy that guarantees their eventual freedom?
Complete Explanation

The key to this puzzle is recognizing that it unfolds over a long period of time. The prisoners do not each visit the room once. Instead, every prisoner is selected many times in a random order before anyone attempts to make the announcement.

Before the process begins, the prisoners agree on a strategy.

One prisoner is designated as the counter. Every other prisoner has one goal: to contribute exactly one count by turning the light on exactly once.

If a prisoner enters the room and finds the light already on, they simply leave it alone and wait for a future visit. Eventually, the counter will enter the room, record that contribution, and turn the light off. Only then can another prisoner make a new contribution.

The counter has one simple responsibility. Whenever they enter the room and find the light on, they increase their count by one and turn the light off. Because the counter always turns the light off after counting it, the same contribution can never be counted twice.

Eventually, every non-counter prisoner will encounter the light off at least once and make their single contribution by turning it on. Since each prisoner contributes exactly once, every time the counter increments their count, they know they have counted a different prisoner.

When the counter reaches 99, they know that every other prisoner has contributed exactly one count. Because the counter has clearly visited the room many times while keeping the count, they can safely conclude that all 100 prisoners have visited the room at least once.

The prisoners can therefore guarantee their eventual freedom, even though the order of visits is completely random.
