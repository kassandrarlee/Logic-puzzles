The Three Gods Puzzle

A puzzle about removing layers of uncertainty in the right order.

Scenario

Three gods—True, False, and Random—stand before you.

  • True always answers truthfully.
  • False always answers falsely.
  • Random answers either truthfully or falsely at random each time they are asked a question.

The gods understand English, but they answer every yes-or-no question using the words "ja" and "da." You do not know which word means yes and which means no.

You may ask exactly three yes-or-no questions, each directed to a single god of your choosing.

Can you determine the identity of all three gods?
Hint 1 — The Language
What is the first obstacle you need to overcome before you can gather any reliable information?
Reveal

The first obstacle is the language itself. Since you do not know whether "ja" means yes or no, you cannot immediately interpret any answer. Your first goal is therefore to find a way to ask questions whose meaning does not depend on knowing the language.

Hint 2 — Uncertainty
Once that obstacle has been addressed, what remaining uncertainty limits how much information you can trust?
Reveal

One of the gods does not answer consistently. As long as that source of uncertainty remains, every answer could be misleading. Identifying a consistently reliable source of information greatly simplifies the rest of the puzzle.

Hint 3 — The Remaining Information
Once those two obstacles have been removed, what information is still needed to completely solve the puzzle?
Reveal

With the language ambiguity addressed and the unreliable source isolated, the remaining questions can distinguish the two consistently answering gods and resolve the final uncertainty.

Complete Explanation

The Three Gods Puzzle is unusual because it is not built around a single insight. Instead, it is solved by removing one layer of uncertainty at a time.

The first obstacle is the language itself. Since you do not know whether "ja" means yes or no, an ordinary yes-or-no question is not enough. Instead, the questions must be constructed so that the same response is produced regardless of whether the god is Truth or False, and regardless of which word means "yes."

One example is a question of the form:

"If I asked you whether God A is Random, would you say 'ja'?"

Questions of this form "wrap" the original question inside another question. Although they may look complicated, they remove both the lying and the language ambiguity, allowing the answer to be interpreted consistently.

Once the language ambiguity has been neutralized, the next goal is to identify—or simply avoid asking—the Random god.

For example, after the first answer has narrowed the possibilities, the next question might ask whether one of the remaining gods is Random, again using the same self-referential form. Once Random has been identified, every remaining answer comes from a consistently behaving god.

The final question can then distinguish between Truth and False, again using the same style of question, allowing the identities of all three gods to be determined.

There is no single required sequence of questions. Several different three-question strategies exist, all built on the same underlying ideas.

  1. Construct questions that remove the language ambiguity.
  2. Use those questions to identify or avoid Random.
  3. Use the remaining reliable answers to distinguish Truth from False.

The elegance of the puzzle lies not in memorizing one clever sequence of questions, but in recognizing that each question removes one obstacle and makes the next question more informative.
