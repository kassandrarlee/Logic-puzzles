Counterfeit Coin Puzzle

A puzzle about extracting as much information as possible from each weighing.

Scenario

You are given 12 identical-looking coins.

One coin is counterfeit. It is either slightly heavier or slightly lighter than the genuine coins, but you do not know which.

You have a balance scale and may use it exactly three times.

Can you determine which coin is counterfeit and whether it is heavier or lighter?
Planning the First Weighing

You only have three weighings, so each weighing must provide as much useful information as possible.

How would you divide the 12 coins for your first weighing?
Interpreting the Result

Every weighing has three possible outcomes:

  • The left side is heavier.
  • The right side is heavier.
  • The scale balances.
What would each outcome allow you to rule out? Can the weighing be designed so that all three outcomes are useful?
Complete explanation

The goal of the first weighing is not to identify the counterfeit coin immediately. Its purpose is to divide the possible scenarios into smaller groups that the remaining two weighings can resolve.

A useful first weighing is:

Coins 1, 2, 3, and 4  vs.  Coins 5, 6, 7, and 8

Coins 9, 10, 11, and 12 are left aside.

There are three possible outcomes:

  • The scale balances.
  • The left side is heavier.
  • The right side is heavier.

The two imbalance cases are mirror images of one another, but each is still a separate possible result.

If the first weighing balances

If coins 1–4 balance coins 5–8, then all eight weighed coins are genuine. The counterfeit coin must therefore be one of:

9, 10, 11, or 12

For the second weighing, compare:

Coins 9, 10, and 11  vs.  Coins 1, 2, and 3

Coins 1, 2, and 3 are now known to be genuine.

  • If the scale balances: coin 12 must be counterfeit. A final comparison of coin 12 against a genuine coin determines whether it is heavier or lighter.
  • If the left side is heavier: one of coins 9, 10, or 11 is heavier than normal. Compare coin 9 with coin 10. If they balance, coin 11 is heavy; otherwise, the heavier coin is counterfeit.
  • If the right side is heavier: one of coins 9, 10, or 11 is lighter than normal. Compare coin 9 with coin 10. If they balance, coin 11 is light; otherwise, the lighter coin is counterfeit.

If the first weighing does not balance

Suppose coins 1–4 are heavier than coins 5–8.

The counterfeit coin must now be either:

  • one of coins 1–4, and heavier than normal, or
  • one of coins 5–8, and lighter than normal.

Coins 9–12 were not involved in the imbalance, so they are known to be genuine.

For the second weighing, compare:

Coins 1, 2, and 5  vs.  Coins 3, 6, and 9

Coin 9 is genuine and acts as a reference.

  • If the scale balances: the counterfeit must be coin 4 and heavy, coin 7 and light, or coin 8 and light. Compare coin 7 with coin 8. If they balance, coin 4 is heavy; otherwise, the lighter coin is counterfeit.
  • If the left side is heavier: the possibilities are coin 1 heavy, coin 2 heavy, or coin 6 light. Compare coin 1 with coin 2. If they balance, coin 6 is light; otherwise, the heavier coin is counterfeit.
  • If the right side is heavier: the possibilities are coin 3 heavy or coin 5 light. Compare coin 3 with the genuine coin 9. If coin 3 is heavier, it is counterfeit; if they balance, coin 5 is light.

If the right side was heavier in the first weighing instead, the same reasoning applies with the heavy and light roles reversed.

Why three weighings are enough

A balance scale has three possible outcomes for every weighing:

  • left side heavier,
  • right side heavier,
  • or balanced.

Three weighings can therefore produce as many as 3³ = 27 different outcome sequences.

There are 24 possible counterfeit scenarios: each of the 12 coins could be either heavier or lighter.

Having 27 possible outcome sequences does not automatically solve the puzzle, but it shows that enough information is available in principle. The weighings must then be carefully designed so that every possible counterfeit scenario produces a distinguishable sequence of results.

The challenge is therefore not simply to compare coins. It is to design each weighing so that every possible result makes the remaining problem smaller.
