Counterfeit Coin Puzzle

A puzzle about extracting as much information as possible from each measurement.

Scenario

You are given 12 identical-looking coins.

One of the coins is counterfeit. It is either slightly heavier or slightly lighter than the genuine coins, but you do not know which.

You have a balance scale and may use it exactly three times.

Can you determine which coin is counterfeit and whether it is heavier or lighter?
Planning the First Weighing

You only have three weighings, so every weighing must provide as much information as possible.

How would you divide the 12 coins for your first weighing?
Interpreting the Result

No matter how you choose the first weighing, there are only three possible outcomes:

  • The left side is heavier.
  • The right side is heavier.
  • The scale balances.
Which outcome tells you the most? What can you conclude from each possibility?
Complete Explanation

The goal of the first weighing is not to identify the counterfeit coin immediately. Instead, it is to divide the possible scenarios into groups that can be distinguished by the remaining two weighings.

A natural first weighing is to compare four coins against four coins, leaving four coins aside.

For example:

1 2 3 4    vs.    5 6 7 8

There are three possible outcomes.

  • If the scale balances, all eight weighed coins must be genuine. The counterfeit coin is therefore one of the four coins that were left aside.
  • If the scale does not balance, the counterfeit coin must be among the eight coins on the scale. However, the result alone does not tell you whether the counterfeit coin is heavier or lighter, so additional weighings are still needed.

The remaining weighings are designed in exactly the same way. Each one is chosen to eliminate as many of the remaining possibilities as possible based on the previous result.

For example, suppose the first weighing balances. You now know the counterfeit coin must be among the four coins that were not weighed. A second weighing can compare some of those four coins against genuine coins from the first weighing. Depending on whether the scale balances or tips, you can determine which subset still contains the counterfeit coin. The final weighing then distinguishes between the remaining possibilities.

If the first weighing does not balance, the second weighing is designed differently. Since the counterfeit coin is known to be among the eight weighed coins—but could still be either heavier or lighter—the next comparison is chosen to separate those possibilities as efficiently as possible. The final weighing then identifies both the counterfeit coin and whether it is heavier or lighter.

The reason this strategy is possible is that a balance scale provides three possible outcomes for every weighing:

  • Left side is heavier.
  • Right side is heavier.
  • The scale balances.

Three weighings therefore produce up to 3³ = 27 different outcome sequences.

There are only 24 possible counterfeit scenarios: each of the 12 coins could be either heavier or lighter than the others.

Because 27 possible outcomes are available to distinguish only 24 scenarios, it is possible to design a sequence of weighings that uniquely identifies both which coin is counterfeit and whether it is heavier or lighter.

The challenge is not simply finding the counterfeit coin—it is designing each weighing so that every possible outcome narrows the remaining possibilities as efficiently as possible.
