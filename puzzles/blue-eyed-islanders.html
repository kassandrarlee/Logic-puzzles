Blue-Eyed Islanders

A puzzle about what everyone knows, and what everyone knows that everyone knows.

Scenario

On an island, there are people with different eye colors. Everyone can see everyone else's eye color, but no one can see their own. There are no mirrors or reflective surfaces, and the islanders never discuss eye color.

The islanders are perfect logicians. If an islander can deduce their own eye color with certainty, they must leave the island at midnight that night.

One day, a visitor publicly announces:

“At least one person on this island has blue eyes.”

Suppose there are exactly 100 blue-eyed people on the island.

When do the blue-eyed islanders leave?
Suppose there is one blue-eyed person

There is exactly one blue-eyed person on the island.

That person looks around and sees no one else with blue eyes.

When do they leave?
Suppose there are two blue-eyed people

Now suppose there are exactly two blue-eyed people on the island.

Each blue-eyed person sees exactly one other blue-eyed person.

Each person knows one of two things must be true:

  • I have blue eyes.
  • I do not have blue eyes.
If you did not have blue eyes, what would the other blue-eyed person do?
Complete explanation

The visitor's announcement does not tell anyone their own eye color. Instead, it creates a piece of information that everyone knows everyone else has heard.

With one blue-eyed person, the reasoning is immediate. They see no one else with blue eyes, so they know the visitor must have been referring to them. They leave on the first night.

With two blue-eyed people, each person considers two possibilities: either they have blue eyes, or they do not.

If a person did not have blue eyes, then there would be only one blue-eyed person on the island. That person would see no one else with blue eyes and would leave on the first night.

But nobody leaves on the first night.

That eliminates the possibility that either person has non-blue eyes. Each now knows that they must also have blue eyes, so both leave on the second night.

The same pattern continues no matter how many blue-eyed people there are. Every night that nobody leaves rules out one smaller possible number of blue-eyed people.

With 100 blue-eyed people, each person sees 99 others with blue eyes and considers the possibility that those 99 are the only blue-eyed people on the island.

If that were true, those 99 people would eventually deduce their eye color and leave on the 99th night. When nobody leaves by then, that possibility is eliminated.

Each person can finally conclude that they must be the 100th blue-eyed person. All 100 therefore leave together on the 100th night.
