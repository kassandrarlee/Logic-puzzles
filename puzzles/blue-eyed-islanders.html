Blue-Eyed Islanders

A puzzle about what everyone knows, and what everyone knows that everyone knows.

Scenario

On an island, there are people with various eye colors. Everyone can see everyone else's eye color, but no one can see their own. There are no mirrors, photographs, or reflective surfaces, and the islanders never discuss eye color.

The islanders are perfect logicians. If an islander ever deduces their own eye color with certainty, they must leave the island at midnight that same day.

One day, an outsider arrives and publicly announces: “At least one person on this island has blue eyes.”

Suppose there are exactly 100 blue-eyed islanders. What happens?

Suppose there is one blue-eyed islander

Begin with the smallest possible version. If there is only one blue-eyed islander, that person sees no one else with blue eyes.

After the outsider says that at least one islander has blue eyes, the lone blue-eyed islander can infer that they must be the one. They leave at midnight on the first day.

Suppose there are two blue-eyed islanders

Each blue-eyed islander sees exactly one other blue-eyed person.

Each thinks: “If I do not have blue eyes, then that person is the only blue-eyed islander, and they will leave on the first night.”

When nobody leaves on the first night, both realize the same thing: the other blue-eyed person must have seen someone else with blue eyes. Therefore each concludes that they themselves have blue eyes.

Both leave on the second night.

Suppose there are three blue-eyed islanders

Each blue-eyed islander sees two blue-eyed people.

Each can reason about what would happen if there were only two. If there were only two blue-eyed islanders, those two would leave on the second night.

When nobody leaves on the second night, each realizes there must be more than two blue-eyed islanders. Since each sees exactly two others, each concludes that they are the third.

All three leave on the third night.

General case

The pattern continues. If there are n blue-eyed islanders, each sees n − 1 blue-eyed people and waits to see whether those people leave on night n − 1.

When they do not, each realizes there must be one additional blue-eyed person. Since each can account for only n − 1 others, each concludes that they themselves have blue eyes.

Therefore, if there are 100 blue-eyed islanders, all 100 leave on the 100th night.

Complete explanation

The key is not that the outsider tells anyone a new visible fact. Every islander can already see that blue-eyed people exist, assuming there is more than one. What changes is that the fact becomes public common knowledge.

Before the announcement, each person may know that blue-eyed people exist, but the layers of mutual knowledge are not guaranteed. After the announcement, everyone knows that everyone heard the same fact, and everyone knows that everyone knows it, and so on.

This shared starting point allows the inductive countdown to begin. The absence of departures on each night becomes new information. Night 1 rules out the one-blue-eyed-person case. Night 2 rules out the two-blue-eyed-person case. Night 3 rules out the three-person case, and so forth.

With 100 blue-eyed islanders, the first 99 nights pass without departures. On the 100th day, each blue-eyed islander realizes that the only remaining possibility is that they are also blue-eyed. They all leave that night.
What this puzzle illustrates

This puzzle illustrates the power of common knowledge. The announcement does not merely transmit a fact; it synchronizes the islanders' reasoning. Once everyone has the same public starting point, the passage of time itself becomes informative.
