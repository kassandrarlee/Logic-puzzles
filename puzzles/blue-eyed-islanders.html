Blue-Eyed Islanders

A puzzle about what everyone knows, and what everyone knows that everyone knows.

Scenario

On an island, there are people with different eye colors. Everyone can see everyone else's eye color, but no one can see their own. There are no mirrors or reflective surfaces, and the islanders never discuss eye color.

The islanders are perfect logicians. If an islander can deduce their own eye color with certainty, they must leave the island at midnight that night.

One day, a visitor publicly announces:

“At least one person on this island has blue eyes.”

Suppose there are exactly 100 blue-eyed people on the island.

When do the blue-eyed islanders leave?
Suppose there is one blue-eyed person

Scale the problem down. There is exactly one blue-eyed person on the island.

That person sees that everyone else has non-blue eyes.

When do they leave?
Suppose there are two blue-eyed people

Now suppose there are exactly two blue-eyed people on the island.

Each blue-eyed person sees exactly one other blue-eyed person.

When do they leave?
Suppose there are three blue-eyed people

Now suppose there are exactly three blue-eyed people on the island.

Each blue-eyed person sees exactly two other blue-eyed people.

When do they leave?
General case

Suppose there are exactly n blue-eyed people on the island.

Each blue-eyed person sees exactly n − 1 other blue-eyed people.

When do they leave?
Complete explanation

One blue-eyed person

The single blue-eyed person sees no one else with blue eyes. After the visitor says that at least one person has blue eyes, they know the statement must refer to them. They leave on the first night.

Two blue-eyed people

Each blue-eyed person sees exactly one blue-eyed person. Each waits through the first night, thinking: if I do not have blue eyes, then the person I see is the only blue-eyed person, and they will leave on the first night.

But the other person does not leave. So each deduces that the other blue-eyed person must be seeing another blue-eyed person. Since each sees only one blue-eyed person, that additional person must be themselves. Both leave on the second night.

Three blue-eyed people

Each blue-eyed person sees exactly two blue-eyed people. Each waits to see whether those two leave on the second night, as they would if there were only two blue-eyed people.

When no one leaves on the second night, each deduces that the two people they see must be seeing a third blue-eyed person. That third person must be themselves. All three leave on the third night.

n blue-eyed people

The same pattern continues. If there are n blue-eyed people, each sees n − 1 others and waits to see whether those n − 1 people leave on night n − 1.

When they do not, each deduces that there must be an additional blue-eyed person. Since each can account for only the n − 1 blue-eyed people they see, the additional blue-eyed person must be themselves. Therefore, if there are 100 blue-eyed people, they all leave on the 100th night.

Why the visitor matters

The visitor's announcement gives the islanders a shared starting point. The information is public, so every islander knows that every other islander heard it. After that, each night without departures rules out one smaller case: one blue-eyed person, then two, then three, and so on.
