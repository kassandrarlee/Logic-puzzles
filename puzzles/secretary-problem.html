Optimization · Uncertainty · Stopping rules

Secretary Problem

A puzzle about when to stop searching when every decision is irreversible.

Scenario

You are interviewing 100 applicants for a job.

The applicants are interviewed one at a time in a completely random order.

After each interview, you must immediately decide whether to hire that applicant or reject them forever. Once an applicant is rejected, they cannot be recalled.

During the interviews, you can determine how each applicant compares to those you have already seen, but you have no information about the applicants still to come.

Your goal is to hire the single best applicant.

What strategy gives you the greatest chance of hiring the best applicant?
The Tradeoff

Every applicant is equally likely to be the best.

It might seem that waiting longer should always improve your chances because more applicants remain to be seen.

At the same time, every applicant you reject is gone forever.

If every applicant is equally likely to be the best, why isn't waiting as long as possible the best strategy?
Complete Explanation

The challenge is balancing two competing risks.

If you begin hiring too early, you have very little information about the overall quality of the applicants. A candidate who appears excellent may simply be the best you've seen so far, not the best overall.

If you wait too long, your benchmark becomes much more reliable—but you also increase the chance that the best applicant has already appeared and been permanently rejected.

The optimal strategy is to use the first portion of the interviews to establish a benchmark without hiring anyone.

The mathematics shows that the optimal stopping point is approximately 37% of the applicants. For 100 applicants, this means observing the first 37 interviews without making an offer.

These interviews are not being skipped because those applicants are expected to be worse. Instead, they provide information about what an exceptional applicant looks like.

After that point, hire the first applicant who is better than every applicant you have interviewed so far.

This strategy balances the competing risks of acting with too little information and waiting so long that the best applicant has already been lost.

Although no strategy can guarantee hiring the best applicant, this one maximizes the probability of success. Remarkably, it succeeds about 37% of the time, which is the highest possible probability under the rules of the problem.
