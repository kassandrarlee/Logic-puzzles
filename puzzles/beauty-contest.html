Guess 2/3 of the Average

A game about reasoning through the reasoning of everyone else.

Scenario

Each player independently chooses a real number between 0 and 100.

Once every player has chosen, the numbers are averaged.

The winner is the player whose number is closest to two-thirds of the average.

Players may not communicate before making their choice, but everyone knows the rules of the game and knows that everyone else also knows the rules.

What number should you choose?
Suppose Everyone Chose Randomly

If every player selected a number uniformly at random between 0 and 100, what would you expect the average to be?

What would two-thirds of that average be?

Reveal

If everyone chooses randomly, the expected average is 50.

The target would therefore be approximately 33.

Now Suppose Everyone Realizes That

If every player expects the target to be about 33, they will naturally begin choosing numbers closer to 33 instead of 50.

If everyone does this, what would you expect the new average to be?

What would two-thirds of that new average be?

Reveal

If everyone chooses around 33, the average also becomes about 33.

The new target is therefore approximately 22.

This same reasoning can be repeated again and again.

Complete Explanation

The puzzle is solved through iterated reasoning.

Every player tries to anticipate what everyone else will choose.

If everyone initially chooses randomly, the expected average is 50, making the target approximately 33.

Once every player realizes this, choosing 33 is no longer optimal because everyone else is also moving toward 33. The expected average now becomes approximately 33, making the target approximately 22.

The same reasoning repeats indefinitely:

50 → 33 → 22 → 15 → 10 → ...

If every player is perfectly rational and believes every other player is perfectly rational, this process continues until everyone chooses 0.

The only stable solution is therefore 0, which is the game's Nash equilibrium.

In practice, however, people rarely continue this reasoning indefinitely. Most people perform only a few rounds of recursive reasoning before making a decision, so real experiments often produce winning numbers well above zero.

This puzzle is therefore about much more than arithmetic. It explores recursive reasoning and theory of mind—your ability to anticipate what other people believe, what they think you believe, and how many levels of reasoning you expect them to perform.

The optimal choice depends not only on mathematics, but also on your model of everyone else's thinking.
