100 Prisoners and 50 Boxes

A puzzle about replacing random search with a path through hidden cycles.

Scenario

One hundred prisoners are each assigned a unique number from 1 to 100.

In another room are one hundred closed boxes, also numbered 1 to 100. Inside each box is the number of one prisoner. The numbers have been placed randomly, with each number from 1 to 100 appearing exactly once.

The prisoners enter the room one at a time. Each prisoner may open at most 50 boxes before leaving the room, all boxes must be closed and kept in their same order, exactly as they were found.

If every prisoner finds their own number, they are all set free.

If even one prisoner fails to find their number, they are all executed.

Before the challenge begins, the prisoners may meet once to agree on a strategy. After that, they may never communicate again.

Can the prisoners devise a strategy that gives them the greatest chance of success?
Complete Explanation

The key insight is that the prisoners should not search randomly. Instead, every prisoner follows the same deterministic strategy.

Each prisoner begins by opening the box with their own number.

For example, Prisoner 5 opens Box 5 first.

  • If Box 5 contains the number 32, they next open Box 32.
  • If Box 32 contains the number 81, they next open Box 81.

They continue following this pattern until they either find their own number or have opened 50 boxes.

Because every box contains exactly one number, each opened box points to exactly one new box. Eventually, this process either returns to a box that has already been opened or finds the prisoner's own number. This creates what mathematicians call a cycle.

Every prisoner who begins within the same cycle will remain within that cycle by following the numbers. If a cycle contains 50 or fewer boxes, every prisoner in that cycle is guaranteed to find their own number before reaching the 50-box limit.

The strategy therefore succeeds whenever every cycle in the box arrangement has length 50 or less. It fails only if at least one cycle is longer than 50 boxes.

It is important to recognize that this strategy does not guarantee success. Because the box arrangement is random, there is always a chance that a cycle longer than 50 exists.

The goal is therefore not to guarantee success, but to maximize the group's chance of success. Remarkably, following the cycles dramatically increases the probability that every prisoner succeeds together from essentially impossible under a random strategy to about 31%.
